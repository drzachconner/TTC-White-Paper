## Three-Tier Evidence Classification System

Every factual and clinical claim in this paper has been evaluated against the following three-tier system. The goal is not to remove content but to ensure that **claim language matches evidence strength** -- a paper can include speculative content and remain credible when the language clearly signals the evidence tier.

### Tier Definitions

| Tier | Label | Evidence Level | Appropriate Language |
|------|-------|----------------|---------------------|
| 1 | **ESTABLISHED** | Systematic reviews, multiple RCTs, standard anatomy/physiology textbooks, widely replicated findings | State as fact: "X does Y" / "Research demonstrates" / "Evidence shows" |
| 2 | **EMERGING** | Single RCTs, well-designed observational studies, peer-reviewed mechanistic research with limited clinical validation, single-lab findings awaiting replication | Hedge appropriately: "Research suggests" / "Evidence indicates" / "Studies have found" / "Preliminary evidence supports" |
| 3 | **EXPLORATORY** | Theoretical frameworks, single studies with small samples, contested meta-analyses, speculative extrapolations from adjacent fields | Signal clearly: "One line of inquiry..." / "Exploratory research has examined..." / "While not yet established..." / "This remains a working hypothesis" |

### Special Categories

| Category | Definition | Appropriate Language |
|----------|-----------|---------------------|
| **MODEL/THESIS** | The paper's own theoretical contribution -- its proposed framework, not a research finding | "In the TTC model..." / "This paper proposes..." / "The TTC framework holds that..." |
| **PHILOSOPHICAL AXIOM** | Definitional statements within the chiropractic vitalistic tradition | Acceptable as-is for the white paper audience. No hedging needed. |

## High-Risk Items Analysis

### A. PEAR/RNG Content (Part IX) -- HIGHEST CREDIBILITY RISK

**Current text (Part IX, "On Intent and Information" subsection):**

> "Emerging research on mind-matter interaction suggests that focused intent may influence physical systems at small but measurable levels. Meta-analyses of studies using random number generators conducted at Princeton Engineering Anomalies Research (PEAR) Lab have reported intention-linked deviations from chance (Radin & Nelson, 1989), with effect sizes small in magnitude but associated with odds against chance of 50,000 to 1. This research points to the sensitivity of complex systems to focused intent."

**Evidence Landscape:**

| Source | Finding | Limitation |
|--------|---------|------------|
| **Radin & Nelson (1989)** | Meta-analysis of 597 RNG studies; reported "odds against chance of 50,000 to 1" | Methodology criticized for potential selective reporting; results "acutely sensitive to the method used to combine the data"; no direct replication of the aggregate finding |
| **Bosch, Steinkamp & Boller (2006)** *Psychological Bulletin*, 132(4), 497-523 | Independent meta-analysis of 380 RNG studies; found "significant but very small overall effect size" | Effect sizes "strongly and inversely related to sample size" (classic publication bias indicator); "extremely heterogeneous"; Monte Carlo simulation showed patterns "could in principle be a result of publication bias"; authors concluded psychokinesis is "not proven" |
| **PEAR Lab (1979-2007)** | 28 years of research at Princeton; observed effects "between one and about 0.1%" | Lab closed 2007; results not reliably replicable by independent labs; two German organizations failed to reproduce; PEAR itself failed to reproduce some results; criticized for poor controls, possible data selection, optional stopping; Princeton administration considered it an embarrassment |

**Risk Assessment:** The PEAR/RNG content is the single highest credibility risk in the entire paper. A single paragraph about consciousness-affecting-random-number-generators can overshadow 30 pages of solid mechanobiological argument. Skeptical readers -- including evidence-minded chiropractors, researchers, and any reviewer from adjacent health sciences -- will flag this immediately. The surrounding "congruent intent" argument stands perfectly well on established neurophysiological grounds (practitioner attentional state, therapeutic alliance, contextual factors) without invoking contested consciousness research.

#### Treatment Options

**OPTION A (RECOMMENDED): Keep in text with heavy exploratory framing**

Replace the current PEAR paragraph with:

> "Some exploratory research has examined whether focused intent can influence physical systems. Meta-analyses of random number generator studies conducted at the Princeton Engineering Anomalies Research (PEAR) Lab reported small deviations from chance in some experimental conditions (Radin & Nelson, 1989). However, an independent meta-analysis published in *Psychological Bulletin* found that effect sizes were strongly and inversely related to sample size -- a pattern consistent with publication bias -- and concluded that the evidence does not prove psychokinesis (Bosch, Steinkamp, & Boller, 2006). The PEAR lab closed in 2007 after 28 years without producing independently replicable results. This line of research remains deeply contested in the scientific community. While it is referenced here as a point of interest for practitioners exploring the outer edges of mind-body interaction, the neurophysiological basis for congruent intent described above -- including practitioner attentional state, therapeutic alliance, and contextual factors -- provides sufficient grounding for TTC's emphasis on focused intent without reliance on this contested research."

**Pros:** Preserves the author's philosophical breadth; demonstrates intellectual honesty; inoculates against criticism by showing the author knows the evidence landscape. **Cons:** Still associates the paper with contested research; some readers will flag any mention of PEAR regardless of framing.

**Required if Option A:** Add to References:
> Bosch, H., Steinkamp, F., & Boller, E. (2006). Examining psychokinesis: The interaction of human intention with random number generators -- a meta-analysis. *Psychological Bulletin*, 132(4), 497-523. doi:10.1037/0033-2909.132.4.497

**OPTION B: Move to appendix with full disclaimers**

Remove the PEAR paragraph from Part IX entirely. Create a new Appendix (e.g., "Appendix B: Exploratory -- Intent and Information") containing the PEAR content with full caveats (as in Option A), plus a note that this material is included for theoretical interest and does not form part of TTC's core evidence base.

**Pros:** Physically separates speculative content from the core argument; the main text stays clean. **Cons:** Creates an appendix that may look like the author is trying to have it both ways.

**OPTION C: Remove entirely**

Delete the PEAR/RNG sentences. The paragraph already has strong neurophysiological grounding for congruent intent:

> "Speaking of congruent intent does not smuggle mysticism into method. Living systems are exquisitely sensitive to boundary conditions and small inputs. Contextual factors -- including chiropractor skill, awareness, confidence, clarity, and focused intent -- demonstrably influence outcomes through well-established neurophysiologic pathways."

This stands powerfully on its own. The B.J. Palmer "that something extra" paragraph that follows also works independently of PEAR.

**Pros:** Cleanest solution; eliminates credibility risk entirely; the argument is actually stronger without PEAR. **Cons:** Loses content the author values; removes a bridge to consciousness research the author may want to preserve.
